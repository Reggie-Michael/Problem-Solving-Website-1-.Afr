            <div class="aquatic-animals">
                <p>
                    <b>
                        Aquatic Animals
                    </b>
                </p>
                <p>
                    Aquatic animals are the ones who live in water. 
                    The aquatic ecosystem provides a natural habitat to millions of flora and fauna. 
                    In aquatic habitat, you will find animals of different sizes, colours, behaviours, features, etc. 
                    Aquatic animals habitat is divided into two types.
                </p>
                <div class="types-o-aquatic">
                    <div class="type-one">
                        <b>
                            Freshwater (Aquatic) animal habitat
                        </b>
                        <p>
                            Freshwater habitats refer to environments characterized by the presence of still or slow-moving freshwater, such as rivers, lakes, ponds, and wetlands. 
                            These habitats are essential for various aquatic organisms, including fish, amphibians, invertebrates, and plants. Freshwater habitats are distinct from marine habitats, 
                            which contain saltwater, and they play a crucial role in supporting biodiversity and providing ecosystem services. 
                            Here's a more detailed explanation of freshwater habitats:
                        </p>
                        <b>
                            Types of Freshwater Habitats:
                        </b>
                        <dl>
                            <dt>
                                Rivers and Streams:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Rivers and streams are flowing water habitats, collectively referred to as lotic ecosystems. They vary in size from small creeks to mighty rivers. 
                                These habitats are characterized by continuous water movement and often feature rocky bottoms, riffles (shallow, fast-flowing sections), and pools.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Lakes and Ponds:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Lakes and ponds are still water habitats, known as lentic ecosystems. 
                                Lakes are typically larger and deeper than ponds. They have various zones, including the littoral zone (shallow area near the shore), limnetic zone (open water), and profundal zone (deep water). 
                                Lakes and ponds can support diverse aquatic life due to the range of depths and habitats they offer.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Wetlands:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Wetlands are transitional areas between terrestrial and aquatic environments. 
                                They include marshes, swamps, bogs, and fens. Wetlands are characterized by waterlogged soils and support a wide variety of plants, birds, insects, amphibians, and other organisms. 
                                They also provide important ecosystem services such as water filtration and flood control.
                            </dd>
                        </dl>
                        <p>
                            <b>
                                Some Examples of Freshwater (Aquatic) Animals are:
                            </b>
                        </p>
                        <div class="example-type-one">
                            <!-- SlideShow -->
                        </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="type-two">
                        <b>
                            Marine (Aquatic) animal habitat
                        </b>
                        <p>
                            Marine habitats encompass the diverse ecosystems found in the world's oceans and seas. 
                            These habitats cover about 71% of Earth's surface and are home to an incredible variety of marine life. 
                            Marine habitats are shaped by factors such as water depth, temperature, salinity, and geological features. 
                            They play a critical role in the planet's ecology, climate regulation, and resource availability.
                        </p>
                        <b>
                            Types of Marine Habitats:
                        </b>
                        <dl>
                            <dt>
                                Intertidal Zone:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                This is the area along the coastline that is exposed to air during low tide and covered by water during high tide. 
                                Organisms here must adapt to changing conditions, including waves, temperature fluctuations, and exposure to air.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Neritic Zone:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                This relatively shallow area extends from the shoreline to the edge of the continental shelf. 
                                It's rich in sunlight and nutrients, making it a productive zone for marine life, including coral reefs and kelp forests.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Oceanic Zone:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Beyond the continental shelf lies the vast oceanic zone, which includes both the epipelagic zone (upper layer with sunlight penetration) and the mesopelagic zone (twilight zone). 
                                This zone is home to diverse species, including fish, marine mammals, and plankton.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Benthic Zone:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                The ocean floor, also known as the benthic zone, includes different habitats like sandy bottoms, rocky reefs, and deep-sea trenches. 
                                Unique species have adapted to the specific conditions of each benthic habitat.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Coral Reefs:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                These are underwater ecosystems formed by colonies of coral polyps. 
                                Coral reefs are incredibly diverse and support a wide range of marine life, from small fish to large predators.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Kelp Forests:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Kelp forests are underwater ecosystems dominated by large brown algae known as kelp. 
                                They provide shelter and food for various species, including fish, invertebrates, and sea otters.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Mangrove Forests:
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                Found in coastal areas, mangrove forests consist of salt-tolerant trees that create a unique habitat for marine life, such as fish, crabs, and birds.
                            </dd>
                            <dt>
                                Abyssal Zone: 
                            </dt>
                            <dd>
                                The deep ocean floor with extreme pressure and darkness, inhabited by creatures adapted to the harsh conditions.
                            </dd>
                        </dl>

            <div class="amphibians">
                <p>
                    <b>
                        Amphibians
                    </b>
                </p>
                <p>
                    Amphibians are a group of vertebrate animals that are well known for their unique life cycle, which involves both aquatic and terrestrial stages. 
                    Their habitats often reflect this dual life strategy.
                    Amphibians are found in a variety of environments around the world, including forests, wetlands, and even deserts
                </p>
                <div class="types-o-amphibians">
                    <p>
                        Types Of Amphibian Habitat for Amphibian Animals
                    </p>
                    <dt>
                        Forests:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        Many amphibian species inhabit forests, including both temperate and tropical rainforests. 
                        They can be found on the forest floor, in leaf litter, near streams, and on low vegetation.
                    </dd>
                    <dt>
                        Wetlands:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        Wetlands such as swamps, marshes, and ponds are important amphibian habitats. 
                        These areas provide breeding sites for many species due to the presence of water.
                    </dd>
                    <dt>
                        Streams and Rivers:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        Amphibians like salamanders and some frogs inhabit flowing water habitats. 
                        They lay eggs in streams and rivers, and their larvae develop in water.
                    </dd>
                    <dt>
                        Deserts:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        While not as common as in other habitats, some amphibian species are adapted to desert environments. 
                        These species often exhibit behaviors that help them conserve water.
                    </dd>
                    <dt>
                        Grasslands:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        Amphibians can be found in grasslands and meadows, especially those near water sources. 
                        They might use these habitats for foraging and shelter.
                    </dd>
                    <dt>
                        Mountains:
                    </dt>
                    <dd>
                        Certain amphibians are adapted to high-altitude environments in mountainous regions. 
                        They have specific adaptations to cope with cold temperatures and low oxygen levels.
                    </dd>
